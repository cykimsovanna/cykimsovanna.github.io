# 5) Wide format for convenience
acs_wide <- acs |>
  tidyr::pivot_wider(
    id_cols = c(GEOID, NAME, geometry),
    names_from = variable,
    values_from = c(estimate, moe)
  ) |>
  mutate(college_grad = estimate_bachelors + estimate_masters + 
                       estimate_professional + estimate_doctorate,
         college_rate = college_grad / estimate_total)

Interpreting the Results

The map above shows that rural counties in Iowa generally have higher median ages, often in the 40–45 range, which can be explained by younger people migrating to cities for jobs or education. As a result, urban areas such as Story County and Johnson County have much younger median ages. It is no surprise that the two largest universities in Iowa, Iowa State University and the University of Iowa, are located in these counties. However, there is an outlier in Sioux County, which is rural but has a younger population than its neighboring counties, partly explained by the presence of Dordt University.

Additionally, the tables show the top ten and bottom ten counties by college graduation rates, which take into account all residents aged 25 and above who hold at least a bachelor’s degree. Not surprisingly, the results show that counties with major universities and urban centers, like Johnson and Story, have the highest college graduation rates with 54.6% and 53.3% respectively, while small rural counties like Lucas and Osceola rank at the bottom with less than 16%.
